Clinical Oncology Agent: Deterministic Pathway Engineering

Scaling multi-drug regimen optimization through a strictly governed, Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG) architecture that prioritizes clinical safety over LLM intuition.

The Challenge: Toxicity-Aware Orchestration

In oncology, the "Planning Rubicon" isn't just about logic—it's about physiology. Generative agents fail in clinical pathways when they cannot mathematically verify drug interactions or genomic contraindications.

Genomic Vulnerabilities

Agents must recognize specific variants (e.g., DPYD deficiency) that make standard regimens fatal. Probabilistic guessing is never an option. [cite: 176-178]

Physiological Guardrails

Regimen efficacy must be balanced against vital metrics like LVEF. Our system enforces hard thresholds at the orchestration layer. [cite: 275-277]

NCCN Compliance

Autonomy is restricted to NCCN-approved templates. The agent can only propose paths within a validated clinical intent registry. [cite: 47, 112]

The Architecture of Certainty

We utilize a centralized orchestrator to turn clinical guidelines into a reproducible execution graph.

1

The Strategist (DAG Compiler)

The system ingests FHIR/HL7 data and consults an Intent Registry to build a rigid Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG). This graph defines the exact sequence of tool calls—from RECIST vision scanning to pharmacogenomic checks. [cite: 47-49, 113]

2

Governance Pre-flight (Policy-as-Code)

Before any regimen is proposed, the Governance Engine performs a pre-flight check on the graph. It evaluates the plan against site-specific protocols using Open Policy Agent (OPA) rules. [cite: 127-130, 269]

3

The Handoff: ZARIF Audit Ledger

Every decision, tool result, and policy intercept is committed to the ZARIF Immutable Ledger. This ensures a 100% auditable trail for clinical review and compliance. [cite: 81-83, 205]
